# Abstract

Multielectrode array (MEA) recordings of extracellular field potentials (FPs) from human induced pluripotent stem cell–derived cardiomyocytes (hiPSC-CMs) are central to cardiac safety pharmacology, disease modelling, and proarrhythmia risk assessment. As the scope and complexity of these assays have expanded since 2022, the methods used to analyse FP signals have emerged as a primary source of inter-study variability. This narrative review surveys key developments in cardiomyocyte MEA analysis methodology from 2022 to the present, using an opinion paper on FP signal analysis best practices as an evaluative framework. We identify a shift from vendor-driven, opaque workflows toward open and transparent software platforms; growing emphasis on physiologically motivated fiducial definitions; structured handling of FP waveform morphology heterogeneity; adaptation of analytical frameworks to three-dimensional cardiac models and computational MEA environments; and expansion into high-throughput and longitudinal applications supported by ultra-high-density electrode arrays and machine learning classifiers. Three methodological gaps are identified as priorities: the absence of a hiPSC-CM–specific rate correction formula for field potential duration (FPD); the lack of formalised waveform morphology classification criteria; and the need for dedicated validation of analytical frameworks in three-dimensional and spatially resolved MEA settings. Addressing these gaps is essential for ensuring that emerging data-driven approaches build on sound methodological foundations.

# Introduction

Multielectrode arrays (MEAs) recording extracellular field potentials (FPs) from human induced pluripotent stem cell–derived cardiomyocytes (hiPSC-CMs) are widely used in cardiac safety pharmacology, drug discovery, and disease modeling (Schulz et al. 2023; Blinova et al. 2025). The field potential duration (FPD) and related metrics serve as surrogates for repolarization and are integral to initiatives such as the Comprehensive in Vitro Proarrhythmia Assay (CiPA) (Blinova et al. 2025). As the use of MEA-based assays has grown, so has the need for consistent, physiologically meaningful analysis of FP waveforms. This review focuses on how **methods and analysis practices** in cardiomyocyte MEA studies have evolved since 2022, with an emphasis on open pipelines, fiducial definitions, morphology-aware handling, and the implications of moving from two-dimensional to three-dimensional and high-throughput applications.

## Motivation: why analysis methods became central after 2022
Since 2022, multielectrode array (MEA) recordings from human induced pluripotent stem cell–derived cardiomyocytes (hiPSC-CMs) have increasingly been used not only for basic measurements such as beating rate and field potential duration (FPD), but also for mechanistic electrophysiological phenotyping, proarrhythmia risk assessment, and longitudinal drug testing. As a consequence, **signal analysis methods—particularly the definition of fiducial points in field potential (FP) waveforms—have emerged as a major source of variability and a limiting factor for inter-study comparability**.

Author's opinion paper explicitly challenged the physiological validity of commonly used FP annotation conventions, arguing that the widely adopted peak-to-peak definition of FPD is often mismatched to the underlying electrophysiology of cardiomyocytes (Mäki 2023). In particular, the first positive FP peak may reflect depolarization of neighboring cells rather than the locally recorded cardiomyocyte, and the end of repolarization should be defined by the return of the signal to baseline rather than by the repolarization peak itself. Broader methodological and biological challenges of hiPSC-CMs as electrophysiological models—including ion channel expression, maturity, and recording conditions—have been discussed from a critical perspective (Schulz et al. 2023). This critique and these perspectives provide a useful conceptual framework for evaluating post-2022 developments in cardiomyocyte MEA analysis.

# Scope and Literature Search

This is a narrative review of methodological developments in cardiomyocyte MEA analysis from 2022 to the present. It does not follow a formal systematic review protocol (e.g., PRISMA) and does not report exhaustive search statistics; rather, it synthesises key developments with emphasis on analysis methodology. Literature was identified through searches of PubMed/MEDLINE, bioRxiv, and Scopus using combinations of the terms "*multielectrode array*", "*MEA*", "*hiPSC-CM*", "*field potential*", "*FPD*", "*signal analysis*", "*beat detection*", "*rate correction*", and "*cardiotoxicity*", with a primary temporal filter of 2022 to present and secondary contextual coverage of earlier work where relevant. An opinion paper on FP signal analysis (Mäki 2023) serves as an evaluative anchor throughout: reviewed studies are assessed against the methodological standards and physiological reasoning it articulates.

# Literature review

## Transition from vendor-defined metrics to transparent, open analysis pipelines
One of the most visible methodological shifts since 2022 has been the movement away from exclusive reliance on vendor-provided "black-box" software toward **open and inspectable analysis pipelines** specifically designed for cardiomyocyte electrophysiology.

Cardio PyMEA, introduced in 2022, represents an early example of this transition. This open-source Python-based application enables explicit analysis of cardiomyocyte MEA recordings, including beat detection, pacemaker origin estimation, beat interval and amplitude analysis, local activation time, upstroke velocity, and conduction velocity (Meyer et al. 2022). More recently, the CardioMEA platform (Lee et al. 2024) expanded these capabilities by providing a scalable analysis framework suitable for high-density MEA recordings and spatially resolved electrophysiological mapping, with interactive visualization and modules for distinguishing diseased from healthy iPSC-CM phenotypes.

Together, these platforms illustrate a broader post-2022 trend toward **reproducibility, transparency, and explicit reporting of analysis parameters**, enabling investigators to critically assess how electrophysiological features are derived rather than relying on opaque vendor defaults.

## Increasing emphasis on explicit and physiologically motivated fiducial definitions
Alongside the development of open software, there has been a growing emphasis on **how fiducial points are defined and justified** in cardiomyocyte MEA studies. The opinion paper by Mäki (2023) describes a concrete, algorithmic approach that reflects trends increasingly seen in recent literature: beat-aligned segmentation followed by averaging across multiple beats to reduce noise, threshold-based identification of depolarization onset relative to FP amplitude, and definition of FPD end as the return of the signal toward baseline rather than the repolarization peak.

This approach contrasts with traditional peak-to-peak FPD definitions that prioritize robustness and ease of automated detection over physiological specificity. Since 2022, an increasing number of cardiomyocyte MEA studies explicitly state whether FPD is measured peak-to-peak, start-to-end, or via derivative-based surrogates, particularly in the context of drug testing and safety pharmacology (Meyer et al. 2022; Lee et al. 2024). Such transparency is essential because differences in fiducial definitions can substantially influence measured drug effects and rate-corrected FPD or QT surrogates. The physiological validity of specific annotation conventions has received renewed attention: Ernault et al. (2024) demonstrated using simultaneous MEA and sharp-microelectrode recordings from cardiomyocyte monolayers that the peak time of the T wave in FPs correlates strongly with APD90, but only when the T wave displays a biphasic pattern. This finding underscores the importance of waveform morphology assessment prior to any fiducial-point-based analysis.

## Rate correction of field potential duration (FPDc)
A methodologically critical step in cardiomyocyte MEA analysis is the correction of FPD for spontaneous beating rate. Since hiPSC-CM cultures beat spontaneously at variable rates, the beating rate can change substantially during recordings. Therefore, raw FPD values are confounded by rate-dependent repolarization effects. By analogy with the clinical QT interval, the Fridericia formula ($FPDcF = FPD/RR^{0.33}$) has become the most widely used correction in the post-2022 literature, applied across multi-centre studies and high-throughput screening platforms (Blinova et al. 2025; Park et al. 2025).

However, the application of clinical rate-correction formulas to in vitro hiPSC-CM preparations carries unresolved limitations. Studies have shown that the beating rate increases by more than 22% and FPD decreases by approximately 8% during a standard 20-minute recording equilibration period, illustrating the sensitivity of FPD measurements to the precise timing of data acquisition (Guerrelli et al. 2024). If recordings are taken before rate stabilization, FPDc values can be substantially biased regardless of the correction formula applied. Furthermore, the exponent in the Fridericia relationship was derived for clinical heart rates of 60–100 BPM, whereas hiPSC-CMs typically beat at 30–90 BPM under baseline conditions, meaning the correction formula may systematically under- or over-correct at the extremes of this range. The absence of a hiPSC-CM–specific validated rate correction formula is a recognized methodological gap that directly limits the inter-study comparability of pharmacological FPDc endpoints. Despite this limitation, FPDc change (ΔFPDc) has become a primary input feature for machine-learning-based proarrhythmic risk classifiers; ensemble methods combining random forest, gradient boosting, and neural network layers have demonstrated improved sensitivity for torsades de pointes (TdP) prediction over single-threshold approaches when trained on CiPA reference compounds (Pramudito et al. 2026).

## Morphology-aware analysis and handling of heterogeneous FP waveforms
Another important methodological development in recent cardiomyocyte MEA studies is the recognition that **FP waveform morphology varies substantially** across preparations, maturation states, and recording conditions. Pacemaker-like signals lacking a pronounced high peak, waveforms dominated by a single peak, and mixed or atypical morphologies are frequently observed in hiPSC-CM cultures.

The opinion paper explicitly addresses these cases by proposing morphology-aware handling strategies, including specialized interpretation of pacemaker-like signals and pragmatic preprocessing (such as waveform inversion) for "only high peak" cases prior to standard analysis (Mäki 2023). While not all post-2022 studies adopt identical rules, there is a clear trend toward acknowledging waveform heterogeneity and incorporating explicit exclusion criteria, classification steps, or morphology-specific analysis branches. This represents a significant methodological maturation compared with earlier studies that implicitly assumed a single canonical FP shape.

## Model complexity: limitations of 2D-centric heuristics in 3D cardiomyocyte systems
Since 2022, the increasing adoption of **three-dimensional (3D) cardiomyocyte models**, including spheroids and engineered cardiac tissues, has further stressed FP analysis heuristics originally developed for two-dimensional monolayers. Experimental studies focusing on hiPSC-CM spheroids have demonstrated that three-dimensionality contributes substantially to FP variability, reflecting differences in tissue thickness, cell–electrode coupling, and propagation patterns (Tsai et al. 2023).

Mutation-specific disease modeling has extended MEA use in 3D isogenic tissues: Maurissen et al. (2024) combined gene-edited *KCNH2* variants (N588D, N588K) with hiPSC-derived cardiac tissue sheets and MEA analysis to recapitulate long QT and short QT phenotypes, with differential repolarization and susceptibility to pharmacologically induced arrhythmias. These findings reinforce the caution raised by Mäki (2023) that analysis frameworks optimized for 2D cultures may not be directly transferable to 3D systems without modification. Consequently, more recent cardiomyocyte MEA studies explicitly report model format (2D vs. 3D) and increasingly acknowledge the need for model-aware quality control and interpretation. Complementing experimental 3D work, in silico frameworks have been developed that couple biophysical electrode–tissue models with phenotype-specific ionic models for heterogeneous hiPSC-CM cultures, enabling computational prediction of drug-induced FP changes across mixtures of ventricular-like and atrial-like cellular phenotypes (Botti et al. 2025).

## High-throughput and longitudinal assays drive broader feature sets
Finally, post-2022 cardiomyocyte MEA studies increasingly emphasize **high-throughput and longitudinal applications**, particularly in cardiotoxicity and proarrhythmia screening. These applications require stable beat detection, robust handling of arrhythmic events, and consistent analysis across extended recording periods and large historical datasets.

Chronic and longitudinal MEA assays have been validated for both acute and delayed cardiotoxicity. Steemans et al. (2023) used 48-well MEA plates to evaluate acute (<2 h) and delayed (≥24 h) electrophysiological and cytotoxic effects of reference compounds—including tyrosine-kinase inhibitors, anthracyclines, and ion-channel trafficking inhibitors—over 4 days, combining field potential recordings with impedance readouts. Park et al. (2025) compared eight arrhythmia-inducing drugs (e.g. E4031, moxifloxacin, quinidine) across healthy and long QT syndrome–derived hiPSC-CM lines via MEA, demonstrating dose-dependent FPD and arrhythmogenic risk and disease-specific sensitivity to hERG blockade. As a result, reliance on a single metric such as FPD has progressively given way to multi-parameter feature sets encompassing beat rate, beat-to-beat variability, conduction properties, and event-based descriptors (Blinova et al. 2025). This shift further amplifies the importance of clearly defined analysis methods, since temporal drift and culture equilibration effects can confound interpretation if fiducial definitions and quality control criteria are not explicitly reported. Guerrelli et al. (2024) quantified these dynamics directly, reporting that beating rate increases by 22.6% and FPD decreases by 7.7% over a 20-minute equilibration window, and that from 2 to 14 days in culture the beating rate decreases while FPD lengthens substantially — findings that highlight the necessity of standardizing recording time points and culture age across laboratories. Hardware advances have further broadened the feature set available for high-throughput analysis: ultra-high-density CMOS-MEA platforms with close to a quarter million electrodes enable spatially resolved extraction of excitation origin counts, propagation area, and conduction velocity maps in addition to conventional FPD, and have demonstrated sensitivity for doxorubicin-induced cardiotoxicity at concentrations an order of magnitude below those detectable by conventional MEA endpoints (Matsuda et al. 2025).

# Conclusion

Since 2022, cardiomyocyte MEA analysis has undergone a measurable methodological shift: from opaque, vendor-defined workflows toward **transparent, physiologically reasoned, and model-conscious practices**. The convergence of open analysis platforms, explicit fiducial annotation, morphology-aware quality control, and model-specific interpretation now characterises state-of-the-art practice and represents genuine progress toward inter-study comparability.

Three methodological gaps stand out as priorities. First, **FPD rate correction remains unsolved**: the Fridericia formula, borrowed from clinical electrocardiography, lacks formal validation for the beating rate range and rate–FPD relationship characteristic of hiPSC-CM cultures. Developing a cell-type–specific correction formula — ideally derived from large multi-laboratory datasets — would substantially improve the reliability of FPDc as a pharmacological endpoint. Second, **waveform morphology classification is still largely informal**: studies describe exclusion rules and morphology-specific handling in qualitative terms rather than through validated shared criteria. Standardised morphology assessment analogous to existing beat acceptance thresholds (amplitude, rate, beat regularity) would reduce between-study variability at the data collection stage. Third, **the transfer of 2D-calibrated analysis frameworks to 3D models and ultra-high-density platforms proceeds empirically**: dedicated validation of fiducial definitions and conduction metrics in 3D cardiac tissues and spatially resolved MEA recordings is needed before these endpoints can be used with confidence in pharmacological settings.

Looking forward, machine learning is rapidly becoming an integral layer in cardiomyocyte MEA workflows — from ensemble classifiers for TdP risk prediction to AI-driven interpretation of spatially rich UHD-MEA data. The analytical rigour established through open-software frameworks and explicit fiducial guidelines will be essential for ensuring that data-driven approaches rest on sound methodological foundations rather than replicating the inconsistencies they are designed to overcome.
